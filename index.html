Who I Am

About Me

I am a Research Engineer/Scientist working with Prof. Mukesh Kumar at the University of Alabama's Department of Civil, Construction, and Environmental Engineering. My work sits at the intersection of ecohydrology, Earth system science, and data science.

My research addresses a fundamental challenge: current land surface models and observational methods still struggle to accurately represent how water, carbon, and energy exchange between the land surface and the atmosphere — especially under environmental stress. I tackle this through four interconnected themes: improving evapotranspiration and soil moisture predictions, enhancing streamflow extreme forecasting, improving numerical weather predictions, and developing early-warning tools for droughts and floods.

I received my Ph.D. in Civil Engineering (Ecohydrology) from the University of Alabama in December, 2023. Prior to that, I completed my M.Tech. at IIT Bombay and held a Junior Research Fellow position before joining UA.

I am also a co-inventor of a novel method (PULSE) leveraging potential water-use efficiency to correct eddy covariance latent heat flux measurements for energy imbalance — a persistent challenge in the field.

Environmental Research Letters · 2021
Retrieving Gap-Free Daily Root Zone Soil Moisture Using Surface Flux Equilibrium Theory
Root zone soil moisture (RZSM) drives plant water stress, drought onset, and land-atmosphere feedbacks, yet continuous measurement across landscapes is infeasible. This study derived a calibration-free RZSM estimation approach using Surface Flux Equilibrium (SFE) theory, validated across 166 FLUXNET sites spanning tropical, arid, temperate, and boreal climates.
Key finding: SFE-derived RZSM matches flux tower observations with Nash-Sutcliffe Efficiency >0.7 at 80% of sites — using only standard meteorological inputs, requiring zero site-specific calibration.

Water Resources Research · 2022
Vegetation Index–Based Partitioning of ET Is Deficient in Grazed Systems
Separating plant transpiration (T) from soil evaporation (E) is fundamental to understanding ecosystem water use. Using eddy covariance measurements in grazed grasslands, we showed that the widely-used EVI-based ET partitioning approach systematically overestimates T in managed ecosystems where grazing alters canopy structure and vegetation density.
Key finding: EVI-based partitioning overestimates transpiration by up to 30% in grazed systems, challenging the validity of satellite-based ET partitioning in agricultural landscapes.

Agricultural & Forest Meteorology · 2022
Influence of Water Use Efficiency Parameterizations on Flux Variance Similarity-Based ET Partitioning
Flux variance similarity (FVS) theory enables ET partitioning using high-frequency eddy covariance data, but its accuracy depends critically on water-use efficiency (WUE) parameterizations. We systematically evaluated four WUE formulations across diverse ecosystems, showing that the choice of WUE formula significantly propagates into transpiration and evaporation estimates.
Key finding: Potential WUE-based parameterization outperforms empirical formulations, especially in semiarid ecosystems where water stress dominates plant physiology.

Water Resources Research · 2024
Structural Constraints in Stomatal Conductance Models Preclude Accurate ET Prediction
Stomatal conductance governs plant water loss and CO₂ uptake, making it the critical nexus of water and carbon cycles. Using physics-informed machine learning across hundreds of FLUXNET sites, we identified fundamental structural limitations in six widely-used stomatal conductance models (Ball-Berry, Medlyn, USO, etc.) that prevent accurate ET prediction under environmental stress.
Key finding: Current models lack key environmental controls and use incorrect functional forms. Physics-informed ML revealed missing sensitivity to vapor pressure deficit under combined water and heat stress — conditions increasingly common under climate change.

Journal of Earth System Science · 2023
Hydrological Impacts of Climate Change on a River Basin Using Macroscale Model H08
River basins in South and South-East Asia face amplified hydrological changes under climate change. Using the H08 macroscale hydrological model driven by multiple CMIP6 projections, we quantified future changes in streamflow seasonality, groundwater recharge, and irrigation demand in an Indian river basin across SSP2 and SSP5 scenarios.
Key finding: Climate change is projected to intensify both wet and dry extremes — increasing monsoon peak flows by 15–40% while extending pre-monsoon dry spells, threatening water security for downstream communities.

Book Chapter · Springer · 2021
Climate Change Impacts on Crop Yield in Upper Godavari River Sub-Basin
Agricultural water demand and crop yields are intimately coupled to the hydrological cycle. This study assessed future climate change impacts on wheat and rice yields in the Upper Godavari River sub-basin using a coupled hydrological-crop modeling framework, accounting for changes in precipitation, temperature, and irrigation water availability.
Key finding: End-century projections show significant reductions in wheat yields (−12% to −28%) driven primarily by rising temperatures, while rice yields show spatially heterogeneous responses dependent on irrigation availability.

Agricultural & Forest Meteorology · 2024  ·  First Author
Are Ecosystem-Level Evaporative Stress Indices Representative of Vegetation Evaporative Stress?
The Evaporative Stress Index (ESI) derived from remote sensing is widely used to detect vegetation water stress and trigger drought early warnings. However, ESI is computed at the ecosystem level, conflating plant transpiration stress with soil evaporation. This study compared ESI against direct vegetation stress metrics across multiple ecosystem types and climate zones, revealing systematic biases in ecosystems where soil evaporation dominates or where vegetation density is low.
Key finding: ESI overestimates vegetation stress by 20–35% in dryland and savanna ecosystems due to high soil evaporation fractions, leading to false drought alarms in food-insecure regions that depend on early warning systems.

Ecosystem Mapping

Understanding the land surface requires working in the field — deploying sensors, calibrating measurements, and characterizing ecosystem structure. This drone footage captures a precision aerial survey of ecosystem canopy structure, used to validate remote sensing data and improve land surface model parameterizations.

Drone-based mapping provides high-resolution structural information about vegetation height, density, and spatial heterogeneity that is critical for accurately partitioning evapotranspiration between plants and soil in complex, multi-species ecosystems.
